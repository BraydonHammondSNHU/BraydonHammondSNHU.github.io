Professional Self-Assessment

Reflections on the Capstone and the Computer Science Program

Completing the Bachelor of Science in Computer Science program at Southern New Hampshire University, and particularly the process of building this ePortfolio, has been the most clarifying experience of my academic career. Where coursework taught me individual skills in isolation — a data structure in one course, a database technique in another, a mobile framework in a third — this capstone forced me to integrate them into a single, cohesive body of work and, in doing so, revealed exactly what kind of computer scientist I have become and want to be. My target specialization is mobile application development, and every stage of this program, from early coursework through the final enhancement of my capstone artifact, has sharpened both my technical ability and my understanding of what professional-quality software actually requires.

Collaboration has been a consistent thread throughout the program, even in a primarily independent course of study. In CS 250: Software Development Lifecycle, I worked through a full Agile/Scrum simulation, taking on sprint planning, backlog grooming, and retrospective roles that mirror how real engineering teams operate. That experience shaped my understanding that software development is rarely a solitary act of writing code; it is a coordinated process of negotiating priorities, communicating status, and adjusting plans as new information emerges. I carried that mindset directly into this capstone, where the code review video I produced for Milestone One was itself an exercise in collaborative communication — presenting technical analysis to an audience of peers and managers who needed to understand not just what the code did, but why specific enhancements were necessary and how they would be prioritized.

Communicating with stakeholders is a skill I have developed through every written deliverable in this program, from project proposals to the journal entries and milestone narratives required throughout this capstone. I have learned that effective technical communication means adjusting the level of detail and vocabulary to the audience: a deeply technical explanation that works for a fellow developer would overwhelm a project stakeholder who only needs to understand impact and risk. The narratives I wrote for each of my three enhancements were deliberately structured to serve both audiences at once, leading with plain-language justification before descending into implementation detail.

Understanding why a structure is appropriate is just as important as knowing how to implement it.

Data structures and algorithms represent one of the strongest technical threads in my education. Coursework in CS 260: Data Structures and Algorithms gave me a rigorous foundation in complexity analysis, search and sort algorithms, and the trade-offs between different structures for different access patterns. I put that foundation into direct practice in this capstone by implementing a HashMap-based search index, three distinct sorting strategies using Java's Comparator interface, and a min-heap built on a PriorityQueue to prioritize low-stock inventory alerts. Each of these was not simply implemented but documented with explicit time complexity analysis, because I believe that understanding why a structure is appropriate is just as important as knowing how to implement it.

Software engineering and database design are the two areas where I have grown the most over the course of this capstone specifically. Refactoring my Android Inventory Manager application to follow the MVVM architecture pattern, introducing a proper ViewModel layer with LiveData, and replacing an inefficient view-inflation loop with a RecyclerView taught me the practical difference between code that works and code that is engineered to scale and remain maintainable. On the database side, normalizing a flat schema to Third Normal Form, replacing plaintext password storage with bcrypt hashing, and implementing role-based access control gave me hands-on experience with the kind of data layer decisions that separate a student project from a production-ready application.

Security, finally, has become a mindset rather than a checklist item for me. Identifying the plaintext password vulnerability in my own prior coursework during the code review process, and then understanding precisely why bcrypt's salting and intentional computational cost defend against the specific attacks that threaten stored credentials, taught me that security cannot be bolted on after the fact. It has to be a consideration at every layer of design, from how a query is constructed to how an authorization check is enforced.

How the Artifacts Fit Together

The artifacts that follow this self-assessment all originate from a single application: an Android Inventory Manager originally built in CS 360, Mobile Architecture and Programming. I chose to enhance one artifact across all three required categories rather than three separate projects because doing so allowed me to demonstrate something a collection of disconnected examples could not: the ability to take a single piece of software through a complete, professional-grade transformation.

The Software Engineering and Design enhancement restructured the application's architecture from a tangle of Activity-embedded logic into a properly separated MVVM design with an efficient RecyclerView-based interface and real session management. The Algorithms and Data Structures enhancement built on that new architecture to add a HashMap-indexed search feature, multiple sorting strategies, and a min-heap-driven low-stock alert system, each with documented complexity analysis. The Databases enhancement completed the transformation by normalizing the underlying schema, securing user credentials with bcrypt hashing, enforcing role-based access control, and auditing every query for parameterization to eliminate injection vulnerabilities.

Read together, these three enhancements tell the story of a single application evolving from a functional but architecturally flat student project into a structured, efficient, and secure piece of software that reflects the standards of professional mobile development. I invite you to explore each artifact and its accompanying narrative in the sections that follow, where the specific technical decisions, the reasoning behind them, and the lessons learned throughout the process are documented in full detail.
